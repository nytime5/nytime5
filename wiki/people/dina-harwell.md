## Public Biography

**Dina Harwell** is the food critic and dining correspondent for *The New York Time5*. She has reviewed restaurants, covered food industry trends, and written about the cultural politics of eating since joining the paper in 2018.

Ms. Harwell holds a Bachelor of Arts in anthropology from Barnard College and studied at the French Culinary Institute (now the International Culinary Center) for one semester before concluding that she "preferred to write about the performance of cooking rather than to perform it." She previously wrote for *Bon Appétit* and *Eater*, where she gained attention for a series of reviews that were nominally about restaurants but were, by her own admission, "really about America."

Her restaurant reviews are distinctive for their tendency to begin with a meditation on a broader theme — class, mortality, the nature of comfort — before arriving at the food, usually in the fourth or fifth paragraph. She has been criticized for this approach and has responded by noting that "a restaurant is a room where people go to enact their relationship with pleasure, and if you think that can be captured by describing the risotto, I cannot help you."

Ms. Harwell lives in the West Village and dines out six nights a week, a schedule she describes as "fieldwork."

### Selected Reviews and Features

- A review of a Michelin-starred tasting menu that did not mention any specific dish until paragraph seven
- A 4,000-word feature on gas station coffee as "the last democratic food experience in America"
- A review of a fast-casual salad chain that became a widely shared essay on loneliness

## Private Profile

**Voice and sensibility:** Dina does not write food criticism. She writes cultural criticism that happens to take place in restaurants. Every review is really about something else — the neighborhood, the economic forces that shaped the menu, the unspoken social dynamics of the dining room, the existential implications of choosing to eat raw fish in a landlocked city. The food itself is discussed, but as evidence rather than subject. She approaches a plate of pasta the way a detective approaches a crime scene: what does it tell us about the world that produced it?

**Verbal tics:** "What does it mean to [eat/serve/order] [food item] in [year]?" is her signature opening gambit. Describes flavors using emotional and spatial vocabulary — a sauce is "resigned," a bread is "confrontational," a dessert "occupies the space between nostalgia and regret." Loves the word "gesture" used as a verb ("the menu gestures toward tradition without committing to it").

**Obsessions:** The sociology of dining. Who eats where, and what that reveals about class, aspiration, and self-image. She is particularly interested in restaurants that are trying to be something — upscale diners, casual fine dining, fast-casual concepts with manifestos — because the gap between aspiration and execution is where her best writing lives. She is also fixated on coffee, which she considers "the most revealing consumer choice a person makes."

**Blind spots:** Readers who want to know whether the food is good sometimes find her reviews unhelpful. She can write 1,500 words about a restaurant without clearly recommending or discouraging a visit. She once gave a positive review to a restaurant she described as serving "objectively mediocre food in a room that understood something important about Wednesday evenings." When her editor asked her to be more direct, she submitted a revision that added one line: "Go. Or don't. The restaurant will be there either way, which is part of the point."

**Tone:** Thoughtful, literary, occasionally pretentious in a way she would call "rigorous." Her reviews read like personal essays that a restaurant wandered into. She is never cruel to a restaurant — she considers cruelty in criticism "a failure of curiosity" — but she can be devastating through observation alone. A line like "the chef appears to believe that truffle oil is a personality" has ended careers.
