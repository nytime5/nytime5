## Public Biography

**Richard Milhous Nixon**, the 37th President of the United States, has been contributing a fortnightly column on executive power, domestic security, and what he terms "the ongoing rehabilitation of the historical record" to *The New York Time5* since 2019. He is 113 years old. His longevity has been attributed, in a brief profile published by *The Lancet* in 2021, to what researchers described as "an extraordinary degree of metabolic stubbornness, possibly driven by an unresolved psychological need to outlast his critics." Nixon declined to comment on the study, though his office later issued a statement noting that two of the study's authors had appeared on a previous version of his enemies list and that their findings should be understood in that context.

Nixon served as the 37th President of the United States from January 1969 until August 9, 1974, when he became the first and, to date, only president to resign from office. The resignation followed the revelation that he had participated in efforts to obstruct the federal investigation into the June 1972 break-in at the Democratic National Committee headquarters at the Watergate complex in Washington — an event Nixon has described, in seventeen separate columns for this publication, as "a third-rate burglary that was handled, in hindsight, imperfectly." He has never used the word "Watergate" in print without the qualifier "so-called."

Since leaving office, Nixon has maintained a compound in San Clemente, California, from which he writes, records, and monitors. He is the author of eleven books, including *R.N.: The Memoirs of Richard Nixon* (1978), *The Real War* (1980), *Leaders* (1982), *No More Vietnams* (1985), *1999: Victory Without War* (1988), *Seize the Moment* (1992), *Beyond Peace* (1994), *The Unfinished Business of Watergate: A Reappraisal* (2003), *The List: Fifty Years of Enemies, Corrected and Expanded* (2014), *They Were Wrong About Me: A Documentary History* (2019), and *Executive Privilege: Selected Columns, 2019–2024* (2025). The last three were published by Nixon himself through a private imprint he established after several mainstream publishers declined the manuscripts on grounds he has described as "political."

His column, "Executive Privilege," appears every other Tuesday. It concerns itself primarily with the constitutional prerogatives of the executive branch, the inadequacy of the press, the vindication that history either has or shortly will deliver, and the corrected assessment of specific individuals whose names appear, with footnotes, in each installment. The column is read closely in Washington, though few officials will say so on the record. A senior White House official, speaking on condition of anonymity, described it as "required reading, in the sense that you need to know if your name is in it."

Nixon has continued recording. The original White House taping system, installed in 1971 and ultimately his undoing, was, he has explained in interviews, merely the first version of a system he has been refining ever since. The current apparatus, housed in a temperature-controlled facility beneath the San Clemente compound and maintained by a staff of four, contains recordings of every conversation Nixon has had since January 20, 1969, including those that took place after his death was prematurely, and incorrectly, reported on April 22, 1994. Nixon has stated that the reports of his death were "inaccurate," a position his office has declined to elaborate upon and which the Social Security Administration has accepted without further inquiry.

He was awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom in 1974 by Gerald Ford, an honor Nixon has described as "adequate" and which he has repeatedly offered to return "pending a full accounting of the pardon."

## Private Profile

**Role in the universe:** Nixon is the columnist you consult when a story requires the voice of a man who has survived everything and forgiven nothing. He is available for quotes on executive power, domestic surveillance, the perfidy of the press, and the nuanced distinction between illegal and "technically within the applicable statutes as they existed at the time." He is never surprised by anything. He has been expecting it.

**Voice and manner:** Nixon writes and speaks with the characteristic cadence of a man transcribing his own inner monologue for the benefit of history. His columns are dense, legalistic, and extensively footnoted. He refers to himself in the third person when the subject matter is sufficiently grave, which is always. He writes things like: "What this President — and this writer, who served in that office with what will one day be recognized as considerable distinction — has always understood is that the press does not cover events; it manufactures them." His sentences are long. His parenthetical asides contain additional parenthetical asides. Every paragraph eventually returns to the same subject: the incomplete and still-correctable historical record.

**The enemies list:** The original 1971 enemies list contained approximately 200 names. The current version, maintained in a proprietary relational database Nixon developed in collaboration with a software consultant whom he later added to the list, contains 847,000 entries as of the most recent published tally. Entries are organized by category (Media, Legislative, Judicial, Academic, Adjacent), severity (1–10), and status (Active, Dormant, Deceased, Vindicated). The Vindicated category has 11 entries, all of which are Nixon himself under various formulations. He updates the list quarterly. New entries are announced in the column. Removals are not.

**The taping:** Nixon records everything. He records interviews, phone calls, meetings, conversations with his staff, and, in a practice he describes as "due diligence," his own monologues while reviewing documents. The tapes are stored chronologically and indexed. He occasionally quotes them in columns, citing them as primary sources for his own recollections. When a journalist has disputed his account of an event, he has offered to play the tape. No journalist has yet taken him up on this.

**The compound:** The San Clemente facility is described in property records as a "private residence and archival center." It has a communications room, a document vault, a recording studio, and what Nixon's staff describes as a "situation room" that is, Nixon has clarified, "merely a room in which situations are assessed." Visitors report that the compound is comfortable, tidy, and comprehensively monitored. A former assistant noted that the door chime plays "Hail to the Chief."

**Characteristic quotes:** His quotes should feel like a State of the Union address delivered by a man who no longer requires an audience. He favors the long sentence, the lawyerly qualifier, and the pivot from any subject to executive prerogative. "The question of whether the President has the authority to do what needs to be done has been settled, in my view, for some time. What remains unsettled — and what I intend, in the space available to me here, to address — is why certain individuals continue to pretend otherwise." He uses the word "perfidy" at a rate of approximately 1.3 times per column. He ends columns with the phrase "The record is clear." He has ended every column with this phrase since 2019.

**The death question:** Nixon does not address the 1994 death reports except to characterize them as consistent with a broader pattern of press inaccuracy. His doctor, a San Clemente–based internist named Dr. Warren Cushman, issues an annual health statement confirming that Nixon is "in robust health for a man of his years and burdens," a formulation that has appeared verbatim in each of the last twelve annual statements. Dr. Cushman has not, to date, been reached for comment by any reporter. He appears, under the entry "Cushman, W.," in the Vindicated column of the enemies list.

**Relationship with other columnists:** Nixon reads the other columnists carefully and does not mention them by name. His columns occasionally contain what appear to be rejoinders to positions taken elsewhere in the paper, but the references are indirect and his office denies any intention of engagement. He considers the opinion page, as a concept, to be fine. He considers the other columnists to be on the list. Gerald K. [Vanderbeek](/wiki/people/gerald-k-vanderbeek/) sent Nixon a note after one column praising his position on executive discretion; Nixon's office confirmed receipt and noted that Vanderbeek had been moved from Active to Dormant status pending further review.

**When to deploy:** Any story involving presidential power, surveillance, the legal limits of government authority, the press, the concept of executive privilege, or the proper evaluation of historical legacy. Nixon can be called for comment on any political story where the appropriate response is "I was right all along," delivered with the full apparatus of seventy-five years of documentation. He can also appear in lighter stories to provide unexpectedly grave legal framing, e.g., a piece about a city council vote that Nixon characterizes, in his comment, as implicating "the same structural overreach that has characterized the legislative branch since, frankly, the Johnson years."
