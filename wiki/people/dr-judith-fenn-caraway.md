## Public Biography

**Dr. Judith Fenn-Caraway** is a licensed clinical psychologist in Ridgewood, New Jersey, specializing in marital and couples communication. She has maintained a private practice in Bergen County for an estimated twenty-two years and holds an adjunct appointment in the Department of Psychology at Rutgers University, where she teaches a graduate seminar titled "Language, Silence, and the Domestic Interior."

Dr. Fenn-Caraway received her Ph.D. in clinical psychology from the University of Pennsylvania in 2002, where her dissertation examined what she termed "conversational non-sequiturs as relational strategy in long-term cohabitating couples." She is the author of *You're Not Listening and Neither Am I: Communication Failures in the American Marriage* (Basic Books, 2018), which spent three weeks on the *New York Times* paperback nonfiction list and was described by *Psychology Today* as "the most depressing book about marriage that is somehow also the most accurate."

She has been quoted in numerous publications on the subject of domestic communication breakdown and has testified as an expert witness in four divorce proceedings in Bergen County Superior Court, each of which she has described as "entirely preventable and completely inevitable."

Dr. Fenn-Caraway lives in Glen Rock, New Jersey, with her husband, whom she has described in interviews as "a good man who occasionally says things that have nothing to do with what we are talking about."

## Private Profile

**Voice and sensibility:** Dr. Fenn-Caraway speaks with the measured, slightly weary precision of someone who has spent two decades listening to people talk past each other. She is sympathetic but unsentimental. She will validate your feelings and then explain, clinically, why they are unlikely to change anything. Her quotes tend to begin with reassurance and end with an observation that is technically neutral but lands like a diagnosis.

**Useful for:** Any story involving marital disputes, domestic communication breakdowns, couples who have stopped hearing each other, or the general phenomenon of two people who share a home and a life but appear to be conducting separate conversations. Also useful for stories about silence, avoidance, and the things people say when they don't want to say the thing they mean.

**Verbal tics:** Uses clinical terminology naturally and without condescension. Refers to behaviors rather than people — "the deflection" rather than "he deflected." Occasionally allows herself a personal observation that she immediately frames as professional. Has a habit of pausing before her final sentence in a way that makes it sound more devastating than it probably is.
