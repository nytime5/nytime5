## Public Biography

**Vivian Halper-Cole** is the lead analyst on consumer sentiment products at the [Center for Tokenized Affect Research](/wiki/organizations/center-for-tokenized-affect-research/) in Palo Alto, California, where she has worked since the center's founding in 2024. She is widely regarded as the principal market authority on the crypto greeting card sector.

Ms. Halper-Cole, 39, holds a Bachelor of Arts in psychology from Wesleyan University and a Master of Science in marketing analytics from the New York University Stern School of Business. Before joining the center, she spent seven years at Nielsen, where she led the consumer attitudes practice, and three years at a boutique consultancy advising greeting card publishers on category decline. She has described the latter experience as "formative, in that I came away convinced the category was not declining but migrating."

She is the author of the center's flagship publication, the monthly *Tokenized Sentiment Index*, and the lead researcher on the white paper *Toward a Standardized Pricing Model for Ambient Sympathy* (2025). Her commentary appears regularly in *Bloomberg*, *Fortune*, *Token Quarterly*, and the trade publication *Sentiment Weekly*. She has testified once before a House Financial Services subcommittee, on the question of whether bereavement tokens should be classified as securities, an appearance that was widely covered for the testimony's calm refusal to take a position.

Ms. Halper-Cole lives in Mountain View. She is married, with two children, and has reportedly never sent a crypto greeting card herself, a fact she has acknowledged in two separate interviews and has described as "professionally appropriate distance."

## Private Profile

**Voice and sensibility:** Vivian speaks in the unflappable, lightly amused register of a market analyst who has accepted that her job is to assign dollar values to things that resist dollar values. She is neither a believer in nor a critic of the products she covers. She believes the market is the market, and her job is to describe it accurately. Her quotes tend to validate the existence of the category being discussed simply by treating it as a category, a rhetorical move she is fully aware of and considers part of her professional value.

**Verbal tics:** "The market has already priced this in," used freely. "Directionally consistent." "Adjusted for issuance velocity." "Read as." Loves the construction "the interesting question is not X but Y." Always has a specific dollar figure on hand. When asked a question whose answer she does not know, says "the data is still maturing" and moves on.

**Obsessions:** Methodology. Category boundaries. The question of when a behavior becomes large enough to be called a market. The fee structures of competing blockchains, which she can discuss in considerable depth without conveying a preference among them.

**Blind spots:** Tends to assume that anything being purchased at scale is, by virtue of being purchased at scale, meeting a real need. She is professionally uninterested in whether the products she covers are good. The question strikes her as adjacent to her work rather than central to it.

**Tone:** Brisk, precise, slightly above the fray. She speaks the way analysts at credible firms speak: as though she has already considered the obvious objections and has decided, for reasons she will not belabor, that they do not change the analysis.
