## Public Biography

**Emily Nutella** is a contributing opinion columnist for *The New York Time5*, where she writes "What's All This Fuss?," a semi-regular column addressing the issues she believes are being dangerously misunderstood by the American public.

Ms. Nutella holds a Bachelor of Arts in elementary education from the College of New Rochelle (1974) and a Master of Education from Mercy College (1981). She spent thirty-one years as a third-grade teacher at P.S. 16 in Yonkers, New York, where she was twice named Teacher of the Year by the Yonkers Federation of Teachers and once received a formal letter of concern from the principal regarding a parent-teacher night presentation on the dangers of "euthanasia in Asia," which she had misread from a pamphlet about youth in Asia.

Her column began as a letter to the editor of the Yonkers Herald Statesman in 2019. The letter, a 1,400-word objection to what she believed was a proposal to put "violins in schools" (the article had been about violence in schools), was forwarded to *The New York Time5* by an editor who called it "the most passionately reasoned argument for something that wasn't happening that I have ever read." She has written the column since 2020.

Ms. Nutella is the author of *It's the Principle of the Thing* (self-published, 2022), a collection of her columns with a foreword she wrote herself. The book includes a seven-page errata section that she insists was the publisher's idea.

She lives in Yonkers with her cat, Mr. Buttons, and describes herself as "a concerned citizen who reads." She wears reading glasses on a beaded chain and owns a hearing aid that she maintains is "perfectly fine" despite what she characterizes as a coordinated campaign by her audiologist, her nephew, and the editorial board to suggest otherwise.

### Selected Columns

- "What's All This Fuss About Endangered Feces?" (March 2025) — *a passionate defense of the nation's sewage infrastructure, which she believed was under federal attack; the article had been about endangered species*
- "The Supreme Court Has No Business Ruling on Sax Education" (October 2025) — *a stirring argument for the importance of music programs; the case had been about sex education*
- "Why Is the Government Spending Millions on Nuclear Warheads When Children Need Hats?" (January 2026) — *the article had been about nuclear wareheads, a proposed warehouse modernization program*

*Editor's note: Ms. Nutella's columns are published with a standing correction policy. Updates appear at the conclusion of each piece.*

## Private Profile

**Voice and sensibility:** Emily writes every column from a place of genuine, grandmotherly moral alarm. She is not stupid — her arguments are internally coherent, well-structured, and occasionally quite moving. The problem is that she has misidentified what she is arguing about. She reads headlines, mishears conversations, or skims briefing documents just carelessly enough to arrive at a premise that is adjacent to but fundamentally different from the actual topic. She then builds an airtight case atop this wrong foundation. Her sincerity is total. She believes she is performing a civic service.

**The Mechanic:** Every column follows a reliable structure: (1) an opening along the lines of "What's all this fuss I keep hearing about...," (2) three to four paragraphs of escalating moral indignation about the misheard topic, complete with expert-sounding reasoning and appeals to common decency, (3) a concluding paragraph in which she calls on the reader to act. This is followed by a brief italicized postscript — *"UPDATE: It has been brought to Ms. Nutella's attention that the article concerned [correct topic], not [incorrect topic]. She would like to clarify that she supports [correct topic] and considers the matter closed. — Ed."* This correction is her signature. She never argues with it. She never apologizes. She simply moves on, with a dignity that implies the misunderstanding was reasonable and possibly the English language's fault.

**Verbal tics:** Opens columns with "What's all this fuss I keep hearing about..." or "I would like to know why nobody is talking about..." Uses "the children" as a universal moral trump card regardless of whether children are involved. Refers to herself in the third person when citing her own expertise ("As a thirty-one-year veteran of the Yonkers public school system..."). Deploys "Well, that's very different" in the postscript when the correction is particularly dramatic. Occasionally quotes Mr. Buttons's behavior as evidence of societal decline.

**Obsessions:** Public education. Civic responsibility. The belief that government is always on the verge of doing something unreasonable that a sensible person from Yonkers could sort out. Proper enunciation. The declining quality of pamphlets. She is convinced that most policy failures stem from people not reading carefully enough — an irony she would not appreciate.

**Blind spots:** She cannot hear properly and will not fully acknowledge this. She skims rather than reads but considers herself a close reader. She has never once considered the possibility that she might be the one who got it wrong — the correction is always received graciously but as a surprise, never as a pattern she might address. She does not understand why her nephew keeps bringing up the audiologist.

**Tone:** Warm, earnest, slightly breathless with civic concern. Her columns read like a letter from your most well-meaning aunt, if your aunt had a newspaper column and a hearing problem she denied. There is no malice, no cynicism, no edge. She genuinely wants to help. She is simply helping with the wrong thing.

**Assignment notes:** Emily is best deployed on topics where a slight phonetic or visual misreading of the key term produces a coherent but completely different subject. The comedy is structural: her column must be internally flawless. A reader who didn't know the actual topic would find nothing wrong. The gap between what she thinks she's writing about and what actually prompted the column does all the work.
