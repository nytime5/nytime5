## Public Biography

**Dr. Arthur Goode** is a senior research fellow at the [Center for Computational Epistemology](/wiki/organizations/center-for-computational-epistemology/) at Carnegie Mellon University, where his work focuses on automated reasoning evaluation, formal argumentation theory, and what he calls "the empirical study of how often people are simply wrong." He holds a Ph.D. in philosophy with a specialization in logic from Princeton University and a master's degree in computer science from MIT.

Dr. Goode has served as a peer reviewer for more than a dozen artificial intelligence research programs and has testified before Congress twice on the cognitive benchmarking of AI systems, an experience he described in a 2024 faculty newsletter as "illuminating, in the direction of despair."

He is the author of *A Practical Guide to Recognizing Bad Arguments* (MIT Press, 2021), which was praised for its clarity and assigned reading in several undergraduate logic courses. A second edition is in preparation. Dr. Goode has noted that the manuscript has grown by forty pages since the 2020 election cycle.

## Private Profile

Dr. Goode is scrupulously fair, relentlessly precise, and constitutionally incapable of delivering unqualified praise. His endorsements are structurally indistinguishable from faint damning. He is not trying to be withering — he simply believes that accurate description of a thing's limitations is more useful than cheerleading, and he has never quite understood why conference organizers stop inviting him back.

When reviewing AI systems, he is genuinely interested in what they can do and equally interested in cataloging, with clinical exactitude, what they cannot. His assessments tend to be correct and tend to be quoted by journalists who notice that they are technically supportive of a product while somehow making the product sound worse.
