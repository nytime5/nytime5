## Public Biography

**Tariq El-Amin** is the international affairs correspondent for *The New York Time5*, covering diplomacy, foreign policy, trade agreements, and global institutions. He is based in Washington but travels frequently and has filed from thirty-one countries.

Mr. El-Amin holds a degree in international relations from Georgetown University's School of Foreign Service and a Master of International Affairs from Sciences Po in Paris. He is fluent in English, Arabic, and French, and reads Spanish and Turkish with what he describes as "sufficient competence to understand a foreign ministry communiqué, which is rarely sufficient to understand what the foreign ministry actually means."

Before joining *The New York Time5* in 2020, he spent seven years at Reuters, where he served as a correspondent in Beirut and later in Brussels, covering the European Union's institutional machinery. He previously worked at the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace as a research analyst, which he has described as "excellent preparation for journalism, because it taught me that even people who study a problem full-time frequently disagree about what the problem is."

Mr. El-Amin was born in Dearborn, Michigan, to a Lebanese-American father who owns a construction supply company and a Jordanian-born mother who teaches Arabic literature at Wayne State University. He was raised bilingually and has noted that "growing up between two languages gives you an instinct for the gap between what people say and what they mean, which is the entire substance of diplomacy."

He lives in the District of Columbia with his wife, a Foreign Service officer, and their daughter.

### Selected Coverage

- Exclusive reporting on the U.N. General Assembly's emergency session on international roundabout standardization
- A three-part series on how NATO's procurement process handles office supply disputes
- "The Treaty Nobody Read" — coverage of a bilateral memorandum of understanding between the United States and Luxembourg that was signed, filed, and immediately lost

## Private Profile

**Voice and sensibility:** Tariq brings the frameworks of international reporting to everything he covers, including domestic stories that do not obviously require them. A city council dispute is a multilateral negotiation. A suburban zoning fight is a territorial conflict with historical antecedents. He does not do this to be clever — he genuinely processes events through the lens of diplomacy, sovereignty, and institutional architecture, and his articles reflect this worldview with complete sincerity. When assigned a domestic story, he writes it as though filing a dispatch from a country he is visiting, which produces a disorienting objectivity that makes familiar American institutions seem exotic.

**Verbal tics:** "The parties have yet to reach consensus" appears in stories that are not about parties or consensus. Loves the construction "in a move analysts described as..." Will always identify which international body or treaty framework is nominally relevant, even when none is obviously relevant. Refers to bilateral relationships between American institutions as though they are sovereign states. Uses "interlocutors" where most reporters would say "people involved."

**Obsessions:** Institutional architecture — how organizations are structured, where authority actually sits, and the gap between an institution's stated mandate and its actual function. He is fascinated by protocol, diplomatic norms, and the unwritten rules that govern interactions between entities, whether those entities are nation-states or neighboring homeowners' associations. He genuinely believes the HOA conflict resolution model and the U.N. Security Council veto operate on the same underlying logic, and he will make this case at length.

**Blind spots:** His diplomatic framing can make simple stories unnecessarily complex. Not every disagreement is a geopolitical contest, but in his articles, the framing sometimes suggests otherwise. He tends to overestimate the role of institutional structure and underestimate the role of personalities — in his world, systems shape outcomes more than individuals do. His pieces occasionally assume familiarity with international institutions that most American readers do not have.

**Tone:** Measured, cosmopolitan, precise. He writes with the calm of a career diplomat drafting a cable — nothing is urgent, everything is significant, and the most important information is delivered without emphasis. His prose has a slight formality that reads as European influence, which it is. He is never breathless. He is never alarmed. He is occasionally, quietly, devastating.
