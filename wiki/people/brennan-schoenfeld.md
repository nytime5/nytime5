## Public Biography

**Brennan Schoenfeld** is the chief executive and co-founder of [Sentigram](/wiki/organizations/sentigram/), the San Francisco-based crypto greeting card company. He has held the position since the company's founding in 2022.

Mr. Schoenfeld, 33, holds a Bachelor of Science in symbolic systems from Stanford University, where he wrote a senior thesis titled *The Economic Properties of Sincerity*. He spent four years as a product manager at Square, working primarily on consumer-facing payments products, before leaving to co-found Sentigram with Anya Korhonen, a former colleague.

He has been profiled in *Wired*, *Fortune's* "40 Under 40," and the trade publication *Token Quarterly*, which named him its 2025 "Founder of the Year" in the consumer category. He is a frequent speaker at industry conferences and has appeared on three separate panels titled some variation of "Beyond the Card: The Future of Saying How You Feel."

Mr. Schoenfeld lives in the Mission District of San Francisco. He is engaged to be married in October 2026, an occasion that Sentigram has confirmed will be commemorated with a custom Wedding Coin minted live during the ceremony and visible to attendees on a discreet screen above the altar.

## Private Profile

**Voice and sensibility:** Brennan speaks in the polished, slightly euphemistic register of a founder who has been media-trained but has not, beneath the training, fundamentally reconsidered any of his premises. He believes that life events are products, that products require platforms, and that the absence of a platform for any given life event is a market gap waiting to be closed. He does not consider this a controversial position. He is mildly puzzled when people seem to.

**Verbal tics:** Refers to bereavement, marriage, and birth as "verticals." Uses "non-trivial" to describe anything difficult. Says "user journey" in contexts where no software is involved. Frequently pauses for "several seconds" before answering questions about whether his products are good for people, then resumes with a different sentence than the one the question seemed to call for. Loves "operational complexity."

**Obsessions:** Closing gaps in the milestone slate. Onboarding non-crypto-native users without their realizing it. The principle, learned at Square, that the best consumer products eliminate the moment of friction between intention and delivery. He believes deeply that grief, like other emotional states, has been historically underserved by the digital economy and that this is, on net, an injustice.

**Blind spots:** Cannot easily distinguish between user need and product opportunity. When recipients respond to his products with confusion, he interprets the confusion as evidence of category novelty rather than category error. He has not, by his own admission, attended a funeral in the year prior to the Condolence Coin's release, a fact he believes did not impair his ability to design for the use case.

**Tone:** Earnest, measured, faintly evangelical. He does not raise his voice. He does not get defensive. He responds to skepticism the way a slightly tired teacher responds to a student who has not yet done the reading: patiently, and with the assumption that comprehension will arrive in due course.
