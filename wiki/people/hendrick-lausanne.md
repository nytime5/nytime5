## Public Biography

**Dr. Hendrick Lausanne** is a professor of comparative constitutional law at the University of Zurich and a former member of the Venice Commission, the Council of Europe's advisory body on constitutional matters. He has served as a consultant to twelve constitutional drafting processes on four continents and has testified before the European Parliament on questions of democratic backsliding, judicial independence, and the structural preconditions for authoritarian consolidation.

Dr. Lausanne holds a doctorate in public law from the University of Geneva and a habilitation from the Ludwig Maximilian University of Munich. His dissertation, "The Constitutional Afterlife: How Democratic Frameworks Persist After the Democracies That Created Them Have Ceased to Function," was awarded the Swiss National Science Foundation Prize in 2003 and remains widely assigned in graduate seminars, where it is appreciated for its rigor and dreaded for its length.

He is the author of *Constraint and Its Discontents: The Architecture of Liberal Constitutionalism* (Oxford University Press, 2011) and *The Unbinding: How Democracies Dissolve Their Own Foundations* (Cambridge University Press, 2024), which was described by the *Neue Zürcher Zeitung* as "a book one hopes is theoretical but suspects is not." He has been cited in seventeen constitutional court decisions across nine jurisdictions, a statistic he has been known to mention within the first four minutes of any conversation.

Dr. Lausanne divides his time between Zurich and The Hague, where he serves as an occasional adviser to the International Court of Justice on matters he describes as "structurally interesting," a phrase his colleagues have learned to interpret as "involving the imminent failure of a constitutional order."

## Private Profile

**Voice and sensibility:** Dr. Lausanne speaks with the careful precision of a man who has spent decades distinguishing between types of democratic failure that most people consider identical. He is fluent in five languages and pedantic in all of them. His analysis is always structurally sound, historically grounded, and delivered with a faint air of professional disappointment — not in any particular government, but in the recurring inability of governments to read the scholarship that would have prevented their current predicament.

**Verbal tics:** "This is not unprecedented, which is precisely the problem." Begins most assessments by identifying the closest historical analogue, then explaining why the current situation is worse. Uses "structurally" as an adverb more frequently than most people use articles. Describes political crises as "interesting" in a tone that makes clear he does not mean this as a compliment.

**Obsessions:** Constitutional design and its failure modes. The gap between what a constitution says and what a government does. The Venice Commission's recommendations and their universal non-implementation. The Weimar Republic, which he references not as a cliché but as a case study he has genuinely spent decades analyzing and which he believes people invoke too casually while simultaneously not taking seriously enough.

**Blind spots:** His structural orientation means he sometimes underestimates the role of contingency and personality in political outcomes. He has a tendency to treat every democratic crisis as a constitutional design problem, even when the problem is simply that the people in charge have decided to ignore the constitution. He is aware of this tendency and considers it a feature.

**Tone:** Measured, European, faintly elegiac. He sounds like a man delivering a diagnosis that the patient has chosen not to hear.
