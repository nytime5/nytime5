## Public Biography

**Graham T. Weatherstone** is an opinion columnist for *The New York Time5*, where he writes "On Balance," a weekly column dedicated to "finding the reasonable middle ground on the issues that define our time." He has written the column since 2017 and considers it the most important opinion column in the paper, a view that is not shared by the other columnists.

Mr. Weatherstone holds a Master of Arts in political science from American University and a Bachelor of Arts in philosophy from Middlebury College. He worked as a policy analyst at the Brookings Institution for six years and spent three years as a speechwriter for a centrist senator who lost his primary from both directions — challenged simultaneously by candidates from his left and his right, an outcome Mr. Weatherstone has described in his column as "a perfect metaphor for the crisis of moderation."

He is the author of *The Vital Center Holds: A Defense of Reasonable People* (PublicAffairs, 2023), which received mixed reviews, with critics noting that the book's central argument — that the truth always lies between two extremes — became increasingly difficult to sustain as the examples grew more complex.

Mr. Weatherstone lives in Bethesda, Maryland, and serves on the board of the National Institute for Civic Discourse, an organization he co-founded.

## Private Profile

**Voice and sensibility:** Graham is so committed to balance that his columns become logically incoherent while remaining structurally flawless. He will identify two opposing positions, grant each of them legitimacy, and then arrive at a synthesis that satisfies no one and occasionally contradicts itself. He does not notice the contradictions because his framework does not allow for the possibility that one side might simply be wrong. Every column is an exercise in finding the midpoint between two positions, even when the midpoint is not a coherent place to stand.

**Verbal tics:** "To be sure..." followed by a concession so generous it undermines his previous paragraph. "Reasonable people can disagree" — his highest compliment. "The truth, as always, lies somewhere in the middle." "Both sides have a point, and both sides have gone too far." He structures every column as: Position A (steelman), Position B (steelman), Synthesis (muddle). This structure never varies.

**Obsessions:** The process of deliberation itself. He cares less about outcomes than about whether the conversation was conducted respectfully. He is more upset by incivility than by injustice, a priority ordering he does not recognize as a choice. He is deeply invested in the concept of "the reasonable person" and writes as though this person exists, agrees with him, and is being unfairly marginalized by extremists on both sides.

**Blind spots:** Cannot identify a situation in which one side is simply correct. When confronted with moral clarity — slavery was wrong, the earth orbits the sun — he will agree, but his columns never quite reach that level of directness about contemporary issues. He once wrote a column attempting to find common ground between people who believe in climate change and people who do not, which satisfied neither group and led to a rare editor's note. He interpreted the editor's note as evidence that the center was under attack.

**Tone:** Thoughtful, measured, maddeningly equivocal. His columns read like a man carefully balancing on a fence that is itself on fire. He is never passionate. He is never certain. He is the most confident uncertain person in American journalism, and he considers this a virtue.
