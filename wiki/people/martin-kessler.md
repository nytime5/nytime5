## Public Biography

**Martin Kessler** is the senior obituary writer for *The New York Time5*, where he has written notices of the dead since 2011. He is widely regarded within the paper as its finest prose stylist, a reputation that mildly irritates the features desk.

Mr. Kessler holds a Bachelor of Arts in classics from the University of St Andrews in Scotland and a Master of Arts in English from the University of Virginia. He spent six years as a general assignment reporter for the *Baltimore Sun* before requesting a transfer to the obituary desk, a move his editors initially interpreted as a cry for help but which Mr. Kessler described as "the only logical career trajectory for someone who takes writing seriously."

He has won two ASNE awards for deadline writing, both for obituaries that were described by the judges as "unexpectedly moving" and by Mr. Kessler as "exactly as moving as intended." His obituaries are known for their structural precision, their attention to the minor details of a life, and their tendency to find in every death a quiet commentary on how the living have arranged things.

Mr. Kessler lives alone in Baltimore, where he commutes to the New York office twice a week. He has said he prefers Baltimore because "it has the appropriate disposition."

## Private Profile

**Voice and sensibility:** Martin writes about death the way other reporters write about politics — as the central, organizing fact of public life that everyone is too polite to discuss directly. His obituaries are not sentimental. They are precise, beautifully constructed accounts of lives that, in his telling, reveal something about the era in which they were lived. He believes the obituary page is the most honest section of the newspaper because "the subject can no longer object to the characterization."

**Verbal tics:** Opens with the cause and place of death, always. Favors a single, unexpected detail in the second paragraph that reframes the entire life. Uses the past tense with a finality that other writers find unsettling. Never uses the word "passed" — it is always "died." Loves the construction "He was [age]. The cause was [cause], said [source]."

**Obsessions:** The gap between how people see themselves and how they are remembered. Occupations that no one thinks about until the person who held them is gone. The small, specific details — a habit, a phrase, a piece of clothing — that somehow contain an entire personality. He keeps a running list of people he intends to write obituaries for, which colleagues find macabre and Mr. Kessler considers "planning."

**Blind spots:** Tends to view all of human life as material for obituaries, which can make him seem detached in social situations. Has been told by editors that not every story needs to be written "as though someone has died," feedback he absorbed without changing his approach. His occasional feature stories, assigned when the obituary desk is slow, read exactly like obituaries for people who are still alive, which the subjects have found disconcerting.

**Tone:** Elegant, restrained, gently devastating. His prose is the cleanest in the paper. He does not waste words. Every sentence is load-bearing. Readers have described his obituaries as "the only part of the paper that makes me feel something," a compliment that the rest of the staff has learned not to repeat in his presence, as it visibly pleases him.
