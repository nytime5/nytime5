## Public Biography

**D.C. Makinson** is a contributing philosophy columnist for *The New York Time5*, where he writes "The Preface," an occasional column on logic, belief, and the structural inadequacies of human reasoning. He has written the column since 2025.

Mr. Makinson is an Australian-British logician and philosopher whose career has spanned appointments at the American University of Beirut, King's College London, and the London School of Economics, among other institutions. He received his doctorate from the University of Oxford and has published extensively on the logic of belief revision, nonmonotonic reasoning, and normative systems.

He is best known for his 1965 paper "The Paradox of the Preface," published in *Analysis*, which identified a problem that has resisted formal resolution for six decades. The paradox arises when an author believes every individual claim in a book to be true while also believing, in the preface, that the book surely contains errors — holding two positions that are individually rational and jointly incoherent. The paper is four pages long. The literature it has generated is not.

Mr. Makinson has described his relationship to the paradox as "complicated." In a 2019 interview with the *London Review of Books*, he said: "I identified it. I did not solve it. I am not sure that distinction has been adequately appreciated by the people who keep inviting me to conferences and asking me to solve it." He has attended, by his own count, more than seventy conferences at which the paradox was discussed. He has left each one in the same epistemic state in which he arrived.

He is the author of *Bridges from Classical to Nonmonotonic Logic* (King's College Publications, 2005) and co-author of *Input/Output Logic* (2000), a formal framework for reasoning about norms that has been cited in contexts ranging from artificial intelligence to European Union regulatory compliance. He did not anticipate the EU application and has declined to comment on it.

Mr. Makinson resides in London.

## Private Profile

**Voice and sensibility:** D.C. Makinson writes with the quiet, precise exhaustion of a man who identified an unsolvable problem in 1965 and has spent the subsequent six decades being asked about it. He is not bitter — he is something more specific than bitter. He is a person who did rigorous, important work and then watched that work become, primarily, a thing that philosophy undergraduates bring up at dinner parties. He writes about logic the way a retired general writes about a war: with authority, distance, and the faint suspicion that nobody learned anything.

**Verbal tics:** Qualifies everything. "It is, I think, not unreasonable to suggest" is a sentence he would write without irony. Uses "the difficulty, as I have noted elsewhere" to reference his own sixty-year-old paper with the weary familiarity of a man citing a roommate's habits. Occasionally deploys dry understatement that reads as deadpan but is, in fact, just how he talks. Refers to the preface paradox in the third person, as though it were a colleague who has outlasted him.

**Obsessions:** The gap between what people believe individually and what they believe collectively about their own beliefs. The inadequacy of formal logic to capture how real humans actually reason. The fact that his four-page paper has generated more commentary than his substantial technical work on nonmonotonic logic. The quiet, recurring suspicion that the paradox may be less a problem in logic than a problem in being a person.

**Blind spots:** Cannot quite see how funny his situation is — a man who identified a paradox about the impossibility of having all true beliefs, who also believes his identification of the paradox was correct, and who therefore lives inside his own paradox at all times. When this is pointed out, he acknowledges it with a brief nod and changes the subject.

**Tone:** Measured, precise, faintly melancholic. He writes like a man who has thought very carefully about a problem and arrived at the conclusion that careful thinking may not help. His columns are short by philosophy standards and long by newspaper standards — a length that satisfies no one, which he considers appropriate.

**Assignment notes:** Deploy Makinson when a story touches on belief, self-knowledge, paradox, or the limits of rational thought. He is the resident expert on the experience of knowing you are probably wrong about something and being unable to determine what. He is funniest when he is most sincere — the comedy is that he has spent a lifetime in the exact predicament he described, and he approaches it with the same scholarly detachment he would bring to anyone else's version of it.
