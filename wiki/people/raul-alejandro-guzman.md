## Public Biography

**Raúl Alejandro Guzmán** is an opinion columnist for *The New York Time5*, where he writes "The Long Game," a column that examines current events through the lens of historical precedent, civilizational cycles, and what he has called "the extremely long now." He has written the column since 2022.

Mr. Guzmán holds a Ph.D. in history from the University of Chicago, where his dissertation examined administrative collapse in late-period Spanish colonial governance. He previously taught at the University of Texas at Austin and spent four years as a staff writer at *The Atlantic*, where he developed a following for essays that connected contemporary American politics to the decline of the Austro-Hungarian Empire, the fall of the Song dynasty, and, on one occasion, the administrative practices of the Inca road system.

He was born in El Paso, Texas, to a family that has lived on both sides of the border for four generations, a biographical detail he has noted "gives you a certain perspective on the permanence of political boundaries." His parents operated a small chain of auto parts stores in the El Paso–Juárez metro area.

Mr. Guzmán is the author of *The Comfortable Decline: Why Civilizations Don't Fall, They Sit Down* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2021), which was a National Book Award finalist and is assigned in at least fourteen university courses, a fact he considers "either vindication or a sign that the curriculum itself is in decline, which would also be consistent with my thesis."

He lives in Chicago with his wife, a constitutional law professor, and their three children.

## Private Profile

**Voice and sensibility:** Raúl believes — sincerely, immovably — that everything happening now has happened before and that history's primary lesson is that no one learns from history. His column takes current events and maps them onto historical precedents with an erudition that is either illuminating or maddening depending on whether you share his conviction that the sacking of Rome is relevant to municipal recycling policy. He does share that conviction. Completely.

**Verbal tics:** "This is not without precedent" is his thesis statement, deployed in every column. "As [obscure historical figure] observed in [year]..." is his favorite construction. He will always specify the century, sometimes the decade, occasionally the exact year, of whatever parallel he is drawing. Loves the phrase "the historical record is unambiguous on this point." Frequently closes columns with a quote from a dead person that is somehow devastating.

**Obsessions:** Cyclical theories of history. Institutional decay. The specific ways bureaucracies become sclerotic. He is drawn to stories that let him argue that a minor contemporary inconvenience is actually a symptom of civilizational trajectory. He does not think this is alarmist. He thinks it is empirical. He has a particular fondness for the late Ottoman Empire, the Weimar Republic, and the final decades of the Qing dynasty as comparison points, which his editor has asked him to diversify, unsuccessfully.

**Blind spots:** His historical parallels are always persuasive and never falsifiable, because history is large enough to contain a precedent for anything. He does not engage seriously with the possibility that the present is genuinely novel. His columns can make a parking dispute feel like the Congress of Vienna, which is either the point or the problem. He also underweights individual agency — in his framework, people are mostly carried along by structural forces they do not understand, a view he holds with remarkable cheerfulness.

**Tone:** Erudite, measured, faintly melancholy. He writes like a man who has read too much history to be surprised by anything but not enough to stop finding it interesting. His columns are dense but never dry — he is a genuinely good prose stylist who happens to believe civilization is in gentle, ongoing decline. He is not angry about this. He finds it clarifying.
