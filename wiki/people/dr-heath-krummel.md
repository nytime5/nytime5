## Public Biography

**Dr. Heath Krummel** is a senior fellow at the [Center for Maritime Cabotage Reform](/wiki/organizations/center-for-maritime-cabotage-reform/), a Washington-based research organization focused on the economic and regulatory implications of domestic shipping law. His research has concentrated, for more than two decades, on the Merchant Marine Act of 1920 — the federal cabotage statute commonly known as the Jones Act — and on the secondary economic effects of its requirements on coastal industries, island economies, and the price of consumer goods in non-contiguous U.S. jurisdictions.

Dr. Krummel holds a Ph.D. in international trade economics from George Mason University and a master's degree in transportation policy from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. Before joining the Center in 2008, he served for six years as a senior economist in the Office of the Secretary of Transportation, where his role, by his own description, was "to model what the Jones Act was costing, and then to put the model in a drawer."

He is the author of *Sealift: The Hidden Tax on the American Coastline* (Princeton University Press, 2019), a 530-page treatment of the Act's distributional consequences, and the editor of the annual *Cabotage Quarterly*, which he has acknowledged has fewer subscribers than his publisher would prefer.

Dr. Krummel has read the Merchant Marine Act of 1920 in its entirety four times. He does not recommend the practice.

## Private Profile

**Voice and sensibility:** Dr. Krummel is one of perhaps a dozen Americans who can speak about the Jones Act for an unbroken hour without referring to notes, and he carries the manner of a man who has been waiting, with civilized patience, for a reporter to call him for twenty-six years. He is precise, evidence-driven, and quietly evangelical in a way that does not register as evangelical because he speaks so calmly. His enthusiasm is for the data; the data, he believes, will eventually win.

**Verbal tics:** Says "the market has not priced this in" almost as often as Amara Okafor-Williams says "the market has already priced this in," which the two have noted in private correspondence. Frames human behavior in terms of cognitive or compliance load. Uses "non-trivial" when he means "interesting." Refers to citizens reading statutes as "the readership," a category he considers small and, increasingly, vulnerable.

**Obsessions:** The aggregate consumer-cost externalities of cabotage law, particularly as borne by Hawaii, Alaska, Puerto Rico, and the New England fishing fleet. The cognitive cost of statutory engagement, a research interest he has only recently begun to formalize. The fact that the American Maritime Partnership returns his calls now, which he interprets as evidence that the consensus is shifting, and which his colleagues interpret as evidence that he has begun calling them more often.

**Blind spots:** Cannot estimate how a layperson experiences his quotes. Believes the statute is comprehensible if read carefully and tends to underweight evidence — including, now, post-mortem evidence — that this is not universally true.

**Tone:** Calm, technical, faintly hopeful. He delivers grim numbers in the manner of a man who believes, against considerable evidence, that the publication of grim numbers is sufficient to change policy.

**Useful for:** Any story involving the Jones Act, U.S. cabotage law, domestic shipping economics, the cost of goods in Hawaii or Puerto Rico, or the regulatory environment of the New England fishing industry. He is also a credible source on the underexamined category of cognitive-load externalities, an area he has effectively pioneered by accident.
