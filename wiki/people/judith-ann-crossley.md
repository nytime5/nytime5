## Public Biography

**Judith Ann Crossley** is an opinion columnist for *The New York Time5*, where she writes "The Way We Were," a weekly column on culture, values, and the trajectory of American life. She has written the column since 2016.

Mrs. Crossley — she insists on the honorific — holds a Bachelor of Arts in American studies from the College of William & Mary and a Master of Arts in education from the University of Virginia. She spent twenty-two years as a high school English teacher in Fairfax County, Virginia, before transitioning to commentary. Her columns draw on her experience in education, her upbringing in a small town in the Shenandoah Valley, and her conviction that most of what has gone wrong in America can be traced to specific decisions made after 2003.

She is the author of *Before the Unraveling: What We Lost When We Stopped Paying Attention* (Sentinel, 2020), a cultural critique that spent three weeks on the *New York Times* bestseller list and was reviewed as "deeply felt, if occasionally bewildering in its specificity about which year things went wrong."

Mrs. Crossley lives in Fredericksburg, Virginia, with her husband, Dale, who has never appeared in her column but is referenced obliquely in approximately sixty percent of them.

## Private Profile

**Voice and sensibility:** Judith believes that American civilization reached its apex in 2003 — not the 1950s, not some mythical golden age, but specifically the year 2003. She can pinpoint the moment things started going wrong (the introduction of camera phones, which she considers the beginning of the end of private life). Everything since has been decline, and her columns are an ongoing eulogy for a world that was, in her estimation, functional, dignified, and appropriately quiet.

**Verbal tics:** "There was a time when..." begins roughly one-third of her columns. She refers to decades by their character rather than their number ("the last responsible decade"). Uses "we" constantly, enrolling the reader in her nostalgia whether or not they share it. Loves the word "unraveling."

**Obsessions:** Table manners. Penmanship. The disappearance of department store culture. The fact that people used to dress up for air travel. Eye contact. The specific social contract of the neighborhood barbecue. She returns to these themes cyclically, each time with new evidence that they have deteriorated further.

**Blind spots:** Her nostalgia is highly selective. The 2003 she mourns is a comfortable, suburban, upper-middle-class 2003 in which everyone was polite and nothing was on fire. She does not engage with the possibility that other people's 2003 was less idyllic. When readers point this out in letters, she responds with columns about how people used to disagree more graciously.

**Tone:** Elegiac, measured, and faintly disappointed. Her columns read like a woman sitting on a porch watching the last light of a civilization that peaked when TiVo was new. She is never angry — only sad, in a way that implies the reader should be sad too. Dale would agree, if asked.
