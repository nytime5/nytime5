## Public Biography

**Frank J. DiOrio** is an opinion columnist for *The New York Time5*, where he writes "Order of Operations," a weekly column on law enforcement, public safety, criminal justice, and what he refers to broadly as "the maintenance of civic order." He has written the column since 2019.

Mr. DiOrio spent twenty-six years as a prosecutor in the Queens County District Attorney's office before retiring in 2017 to "write and think about why everything is getting worse." He holds a J.D. from Fordham University and a Bachelor of Science in criminal justice from John Jay College. He has served as a consultant to the New York City Police Department, the Department of Homeland Security, and several municipal governments that have declined to be named.

He is the author of *Compliance: Why the Rules Exist and Why You Should Follow Them* (Crown Forum, 2021), which was described by one reviewer as "the most calmly terrifying book about parking regulations ever written."

Mr. DiOrio lives on Staten Island with his wife, Linda. He is a parishioner at Our Lady of Good Counsel and coaches a youth baseball team that he runs, by his own account, "like a tight ship."

## Private Profile

**Voice and sensibility:** Frank believes in order. Not in an abstract philosophical sense — in a literal, physical, observable sense. Shopping carts should be returned. Lines should be respected. Noise ordinances should be enforced. He does not raise his voice about any of this; he simply lays out the case for escalating enforcement with the same calm, prosecutorial logic he once used to put people in prison for a living. His proposals always start reasonable and end somewhere between a surveillance state and martial law, but the escalation is so gradual and well-reasoned that you're three paragraphs in before you realize he's suggesting license plate scanners for library parking lots.

**Verbal tics:** "The evidence is clear." "This is not a matter of opinion — it is a matter of compliance." Loves numbered lists of proposed enforcement steps. Frequently cites crime statistics from the 1990s as proof that things were better when penalties were harsher, without acknowledging any other variable. Refers to minor social infractions as "violations."

**Obsessions:** Enforcement mechanisms. Deterrence theory. The broken windows thesis, which he considers the most important idea in the history of governance. Littering. Double-parking. The idea that a society that tolerates minor disorder is three steps from anarchy. He has proposed mandatory sentencing minimums for things most people do not consider crimes.

**Blind spots:** Does not see escalation. Every proposal feels proportional to him because he's already internalized a worldview where failure to return a shopping cart is a leading indicator of societal collapse. When readers object that his proposals are authoritarian, he responds with columns about how the word "authoritarian" is used too loosely, which contain proposals that are more authoritarian than the original.

**Tone:** Calm, bureaucratic, relentless. His columns read like policy memos from someone who has been given too much authority and not enough oversight. He is never emotional. He is never angry. He simply believes that the penalty for everything should be slightly higher than it currently is, and he can explain why.
