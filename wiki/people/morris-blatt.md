## Public Biography

**Morris Blatt** is a senior partner at Blatt, Grunewald & Peck, a boutique litigation firm in Newark, New Jersey, that specializes in what Mr. Blatt describes as "ancestral intellectual property" — legal claims arising from the uncompensated use of a deceased person's ideas, innovations, or creative output by subsequent generations.

Mr. Blatt received his J.D. from Seton Hall University School of Law in 1997 and practiced general commercial litigation for fifteen years before pivoting to his current specialty. He has said that the pivot was inspired by a 2012 case in which he represented the grandchildren of a plumber who had invented a particular valve fitting in 1948, only to see the design adopted industry-wide without credit. The case was dismissed, but Mr. Blatt described the experience as "formative."

He is the author of a self-published legal monograph, *The Inheritance of Ideas: A Framework for Multigenerational Intellectual Property Rights* (2019), which proposes that intellectual contributions should be treated as heritable assets in perpetuity. The monograph has been cited in zero judicial opinions and reviewed in one legal blog, which called it "creative."

Mr. Blatt is representing [Greta Einstein-Haas](/wiki/people/greta-einstein-haas/) in her lawsuit against approximately 40,000 physicists, which he has described as "the most important intellectual property case since the invention of intellectual property."

He lives in Maplewood, New Jersey, with his wife, a school librarian, and their three cats.

## Private Profile

**Voice and sensibility:** Morris Blatt is a true believer. He did not take this case for publicity or for the prospect of a settlement — he took it because he genuinely believes that the legal system has failed to account for the multigenerational value of foundational ideas. He speaks about ancestral IP with the quiet intensity of a man who has been thinking about this for a very long time and is relieved that someone has finally given him a case large enough to prove his theory.

**Verbal tics:** Loves real-estate metaphors for intellectual property ("If you build a house on someone's land..."). Uses "my client's ancestor" rather than "Einstein," which makes it sound more like a property dispute and less like a philosophical argument. Frequently says "the legal principle is sound" in contexts where it demonstrably is not.

**Obsessions:** The concept that ideas, once generated, remain the property of the mind that generated them — and by extension, of that mind's descendants. He views the public domain not as a legal concept but as a form of theft that has been normalized through repetition.

**Blind spots:** Entirely unbothered by the fact that no court has ever recognized his legal theory. Interprets this as evidence that the theory is ahead of its time rather than that it is wrong. Also does not seem to have considered that Einstein himself built on prior work, a point he deflects by noting that "Newton's descendants are welcome to file their own suit."

**Tone:** Professionally confident, slightly grandiose. Speaks to reporters the way a district attorney speaks about an airtight case, except the case has no legal foundation and he does not appear to notice.
