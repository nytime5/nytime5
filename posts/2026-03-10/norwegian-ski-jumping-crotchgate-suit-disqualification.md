OBERSTDORF, Germany — The Norwegian women's ski jumping team was disqualified from the normal hill team competition at the 2021 FIS Nordic World Ski Championships on Thursday after equipment inspectors determined that the groin sac of each competitor's jumpsuit exceeded the maximum permitted crotch panel dimensions by two centimeters, officials from the International Ski Federation confirmed, igniting a controversy that would come to be known, in sports media and regulatory circles alike, as Crotchgate.

The four Norwegian athletes — Silje Opseth, Anna Odine Strøm, Thea Minyan Bjørseth, and Maren Lundby, the reigning Olympic champion — were removed from the results following a post-competition suit inspection in which officials applied a standardized tape measure to the nether pouch region of each jumpsuit and found, in each case, a crotch gusset that fell outside the tolerances set forth in Section 4.2 of the FIS Ski Jumping Equipment Regulations. Under those rules, a jumpsuit's groin bag must conform closely to the athlete's body; excess volume in the perineal sac area is prohibited on the grounds that it may create an aerodynamic advantage, allowing the billowing crotch sack to function, in effect, as an additional lifting surface during flight. Austrian jumper Sara Takanashi, the world's most decorated female ski jumper, was separately disqualified on the same grounds, her own groin pouch likewise deemed non-compliant.

"The measurement is very clear," said Hans-Peter Gröber, a senior technical delegate for the FIS, in a statement issued Friday. "The allowable deviation in the crotch zone of the suit is zero to two centimeters. In the cases reviewed, the crotch pocket of each garment exceeded that threshold. We understand this outcome is disappointing. The rules, however, exist for the integrity of the sport, and the integrity of the sport begins with the integrity of the crotch panel." Norwegian Ski Federation President Erik Røste disputed the methodology, stating in a separate release that officials had pulled the suit's scrotal gusset taut during measurement rather than allowing the crotch bag to rest in its natural position against the body, thereby inflating the recorded figure and producing an artificially noncompliant result. The FIS rejected that characterization.

The disqualification handed Germany, whose athletes' crotch dimensions were found to be within regulation, a gold medal in the team event. The result stood. Dr. Miriam Sandvik, a sports governance researcher at the University of Oslo and the author of a 2019 monograph on aerodynamic regulation in Nordic disciplines, said the episode had exposed the degree to which the outcome of elite competition could hinge on a measurement taken in a fluorescent-lit corridor with a cloth tape. "Two centimeters in the crotch sac," Dr. Sandvik said. "That is the margin by which a World Championship was decided." She added that the FIS had for years resisted calls to standardize the crotch-inspection protocol across member nations, and that the ambiguity in how inspectors were trained to hold the groin pouch during measurement had created what she described as "a structural vulnerability in the regulatory framework." The FIS said it would review its crotch-measurement procedures in advance of the following season.
