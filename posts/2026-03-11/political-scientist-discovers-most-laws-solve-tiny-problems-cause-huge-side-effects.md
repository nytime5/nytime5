WASHINGTON — A Georgetown University political scientist has published what colleagues are calling the most comprehensive analysis of congressional lawmaking in a generation, and its central finding is one that practitioners on both sides of the aisle have long suspected but never quantified: virtually every law passed by Congress solves a problem so small it barely exists while producing side effects so large they reshape the lives of millions of people who were not previously aware they had a stake in the matter.

Dr. Raymond Ulrich, who spent nine years cataloging the stated objectives and actual outcomes of every piece of legislation signed into law between 1981 and 2024, said the pattern was so consistent that he initially believed his methodology was flawed. "I kept rechecking the data because it seemed impossible that it could be this uniform," he said. "But it is. Congress identifies a problem affecting a tiny number of people, drafts a solution that nominally addresses it, and then attaches provisions that impose sweeping burdens on the opposing party's constituents. Every single time."

The study, published this month in the *American Political Science Review*, introduces what Dr. Ulrich calls the Legislative Harm Ratio, a metric comparing the number of people helped by a law's stated purpose to the number of people negatively affected by its secondary consequences. The median ratio across more than four thousand pieces of legislation was 1 to 11,400, meaning that for every individual whose original problem was solved, approximately eleven thousand others experienced a new one.

"Take the Residential Mailbox Visibility Act of 2009," Dr. Ulrich said, citing one of the study's more vivid examples. "It was introduced to address complaints from fourteen postal carriers in rural Oregon who could not see certain mailboxes in heavy fog. The law itself mandated reflective tape on all residential mailboxes nationwide. Harmless enough. But a rider attached in committee restructured rural broadband subsidies in a way that increased internet costs for six million households in states that had voted for the opposing party in the previous election."

Former Representative Dale Koestner, who served twelve terms in Congress before retiring in 2022, said the findings were unsurprising. "You learn very quickly that the stated purpose of a bill is like the parsley on the plate," he said. "Nobody is there for the parsley. The entrée is on page forty-seven, and it usually involves making life incrementally worse for people your donors do not like."

Dr. Ulrich's research also found that the pattern is bipartisan and symmetrical. Democratic and Republican legislators produce nearly identical harm ratios, differing only in which populations absorb the collateral damage. "Each side has essentially perfected the art of solving a problem nobody has while creating problems everybody has," he said. "It is, in a certain light, the most efficient system imaginable. Just not in the way anyone would want."

Senator Margaret Liu of Colorado, one of the few sitting members of Congress willing to comment on the study, disputed its framing while largely confirming its findings. "I would not say we cause harm," she said. "I would say we redistribute inconvenience in a targeted fashion. That is what governance is."

Dr. Ulrich said he plans to expand the study to include state legislatures, though early results suggest the pattern holds at every level of government. "The scale is smaller, but the ratio is the same," he said. "A city council will pass an ordinance to fix a pothole that one person complained about, and somehow it ends up raising parking fees for an entire zip code that voted the wrong way. It is a fractal."
