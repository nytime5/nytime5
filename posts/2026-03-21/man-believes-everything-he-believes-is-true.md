MADISON, Wis. — The kitchen table is clean except for a legal pad, a ballpoint pen, and a mug of coffee that Noel Stettner poured forty minutes ago and has not yet touched. He is writing, slowly and with evident care, a sentence that he has written before. He looks at it. He crosses it out. He writes it again. It is, by any measure, a difficult morning.

Mr. Stettner, 53, a database administrator for the Dane County Department of Public Works, has spent the past eleven weeks confronting what he describes as a "situation" in his own thinking. He believes everything he believes is true. He also believes, with equal and unwavering conviction, that it is not possible for a person to believe only true things. He has been unable to resolve this. He does not expect to.

"I'm not saying I'm special," Mr. Stettner said, sitting at the table where he has spent most of his mornings since mid-January. "I just happen to believe that all my beliefs are accurate. But I also believe that nobody's beliefs are all accurate. Including mine. So." He paused. "So."

The difficulty, as Mr. Stettner describes it, is not that he holds contradictory positions. It is that he holds each position for what he considers excellent reasons. He believes the earth is round, that Madison's recycling program is underfunded, that his brother-in-law should not have bought a timeshare, and that water freezes at thirty-two degrees Fahrenheit. He can identify no belief of his own that he considers false — because, he notes, if he thought it was false, he would stop believing it, and then it would no longer be a belief. He finds this airtight. He also finds it suspicious.

[Dr. Arthur Goode](/wiki/people/dr-arthur-goode/), a senior research fellow at the [Center for Computational Epistemology](/wiki/organizations/center-for-computational-epistemology/) at Carnegie Mellon University, said that Mr. Stettner's predicament is, in formal terms, well-documented. "This is a known problem in epistemology," Dr. Goode said by phone. "It is sometimes called the preface paradox. A person can rationally believe each individual claim in a book while also believing that the book, taken as a whole, contains at least one error. The two positions are individually reasonable and jointly incoherent. It's been studied extensively." He paused. "It has not been solved."

Dr. Goode said the center's database of formally cataloged epistemic failures contains more than four hundred entries related to the paradox, including several submitted by researchers who acknowledged, in their cover letters, that their own analyses might contain errors.

Mr. Stettner said he first became aware of the problem in January, during a conversation with his daughter, Megan, a sophomore at the University of Wisconsin studying philosophy. She had mentioned the concept of epistemic humility — the idea that a rational person should expect some of their beliefs to be wrong, even if they cannot identify which ones. Mr. Stettner agreed immediately. Then he went home and tried to identify one of his own beliefs that might be wrong. He could not find one. He has been looking since.

"I went through them methodically," he said. He produced a legal pad on which he had listed, by category, every belief he could articulate. There were sections for politics, science, personal finance, sports predictions, and a category labeled "Gut Feelings, Strong." Each entry had a checkmark beside it. "Every single one of these is correct," he said. "I'm sure of it. And I'm also sure that can't be right."

His wife, Laura Stettner, said the situation has been manageable but persistent. "He's fine at work. He's fine at dinner. But if you ask him a question — any question — you can see him sort of check it twice now," she said. "He told me yesterday he believes the dishwasher is running. And then he just stood there for a minute."

Dr. Goode said that the practical effects of the paradox are typically minimal. "Most people who encounter this problem do what any rational person would do," he said. "They acknowledge it abstractly and then continue to believe everything they believe with undiminished confidence. The unusual thing about Mr. Stettner is that he appears to be taking it seriously."

Mr. Stettner said he has considered the possibility that the belief causing the problem is the belief that not all his beliefs can be true. If he abandoned that belief, the paradox would collapse. But he believes that belief is also true. He is, by his own assessment, stuck.

"The worst part," he said, looking at the legal pad, "is that I believe I'll figure this out eventually." He set down the pen. "And I also believe that I won't."
