title: "Leading Philosophers Concede That if Biological Sex Is Not Rooted in Objective Reality, Then Nothing Is, Which They Acknowledge Is Actually a Coherent Position"
date: 2026-03-09
tags: [philosophy, academia, epistemology, biology]
excerpt: "The nation's foremost metaphysicians have asked only that everyone involved be honest about what they are signing up for."
---

CAMBRIDGE, Mass. — A coalition of prominent analytic philosophers released a joint statement on Monday acknowledging that if the claim "biological sex is not grounded in objective reality" is to be taken seriously, then the logical consequence is that nothing at all is grounded in objective reality — a position they described as "ancient, internally consistent, and frankly underappreciated."

"We want to be very clear that radical ontological anti-realism is a perfectly defensible philosophical stance with a rich intellectual tradition stretching back to Pyrrho of Elis," said Dr. Margaret Ashworth, chair of the Department of Philosophy at Harvard University. "What we object to is people arriving at it accidentally and then refusing to admit where they are standing."

The statement, signed by forty-three tenured philosophers across twelve institutions, argues that if empirically observable, chromosomally verifiable, and reproductively consequential biological categories do not qualify as objective features of reality, then the bar for objectivity has been set at a height that no claim about any phenomenon — including gravity, photosynthesis, or the existence of other minds — can clear. The authors stressed that they found this conclusion not alarming but "clarifying."

"You are welcome to deny the objectivity of sex," said Dr. Theodore Balk, a professor of metaphysics at the University of Chicago. "But you must then, in good conscience, also deny the objectivity of chemistry, geology, mammalian taxonomy, and whether it is currently raining. You cannot selectively apply radical skepticism to one empirical category and then conduct the rest of your life as a naïve realist. That is not philosophy. That is convenience."

Critics of the statement accused the philosophers of engaging in a slippery-slope argument, to which the authors responded that the slope is not slippery but "perfectly flat and already at the bottom." Dr. Ashworth noted that she had fielded numerous objections from colleagues in other departments who insisted that sex was a special case, distinct from other biological categories in ways they declined to specify.

"Several people have told me that this is different because it is political," Dr. Ashworth said. "I have asked each of them to point me to the section of the metaphysics literature where political salience alters the ontological status of a phenomenon. So far I have received no citations."

The American Philosophical Association has scheduled a panel discussion on the matter for its annual conference in April, titled "If This Isn't Real, What Is? An Honest Accounting." Registration is expected to be contentious.
